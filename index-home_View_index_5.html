Wang Ximeng was a painter of the Northern Song dynasty, born in 1096.

During the Zhenghe reign (1111–1117 CE) of the Northern Song, he was a student at the Imperial Painting Academy, and was later summoned to work in the Imperial Library. Initially, his skills were not highly developed. Emperor Huizong (Zhao Ji), who served as a master at the Painting Academy at the time, recognized his exceptional talent, declaring, “This young man is teachable.” He then personally instructed Wang Ximeng in the principles of painting. Under the direct guidance of Emperor Huizong, Wang’s artistic skills advanced rapidly.In the fourth month of the third year of the Zhenghe reign (1113 CE), at the age of 18, Wang Ximeng completed the handscroll One Thousand Li of Rivers and Mountains over a period of six months. He died young shortly thereafter.


Medium and Dimensions

Executed in color on silk, the scroll measures 51.5 cm in height and 1191.5 cm in length. It is recognized as the longest surviving landscape handscroll from the Northern Song dynasty.

Artistic Features

Employing the Da Qinglü (Great Blue-and-Green) coloration technique, the painting features vivid and highly decorative hues. It masterfully captures the grandeur and elegance of the landscape, standing as a preeminent masterpiece of the blue-and-green landscape genre.

Artistic Achievement of the Artist

Wang Ximeng secured his pivotal position in the history of Chinese painting with One Thousand Li of Rivers and Mountains—his only known surviving work. He is celebrated as a prodigious young artist renowned for creating a timeless masterpiece single-handedly.

Artistic Techniques and Aesthetic Features of One Thousand Li of Rivers and Mountains

1. Coloration and Style

The work employs the quintessential techniques of blue-and-green landscape painting. Dominated by mineral pigments of azurite and malachite, and complemented by resplendent mineral colors, it creates a visual effect that is both opulent and refreshing, fully embodying the magnificent and luxurious aesthetic of Song dynasty court painting.

2. Color and Atmosphere

The painting exhibits distinct color layers, with striking contrasts that are harmoniously unified. This not only accentuates the lush, vibrant green of the mountains and rivers but also imbues the composition with an otherworldly, transcendent beauty.

3. Brushwork and Execution

The brushwork is delicate yet forceful. Whether rendering the intricate textures of rocks and mountains, or the delicate details of trees, dwellings, and human figures, the execution is masterfully balanced. It seamlessly integrates the meticulous precision of gongbi (fine-line painting) with the expressive vitality of xieyi (freehand brushwork), demonstrating the artist’s consummate skill and acute observation of nature.
