Yan Liben (active 7th century, d. 673 CE, fourth year of the Xianheng reign of Emperor Gaozong of Tang) was a celebrated painter of the early Tang Dynasty. Born into an artistically inclined family, his father and elder brother were both court artists skilled in painting and design. After his brother’s death, Yan succeeded him at the imperial court. His artistic career was closely tied to Li Shimin (Emperor Taizong of Tang): he served in Li’s household when the latter was still Prince of Qin, and in 626 CE painted portraits of the 18 strategists who had helped Li seize power. In 643 CE, by which time Li had ascended the throne, Yan was commissioned to paint portraits of 24 founding statesmen of the Tang Dynasty for display in Lingyan Pavilion, a major imperial hall. These works, known as Portraits of Meritorious Statesmen at Lingyan Pavilion, showcased his exceptional talent. Yan rose to high office at court, eventually serving as Right Chancellor. His contemporary, the Left Chancellor, was a military general renowned for frontier campaigns, leading to the popular saying: “The Left Chancellor spreads his might across the deserts, while the Right Chancellor earns renown through his brush and ink.”

"Bu Nian Tu”is a silk colour painting by Yan Liben of the Tang Dynasty, now preserved in the Palace Museum, Beijing, as one of China’s top ten celebrated classical paintings. The original scroll has been lost after more than a thousand years of transmission, and the extant version is a Song Dynasty copy. It is 38.5 cm in height and 129.6 cm in width, with no artist’s signature.

In 640 CE (the 14th year of the Zhenguan reign), Songtsen Gampo, ruler of the Tibetan Empire, sent his envoy Gar Tongtsen Yülsung to Chang’an to propose a dynastic marriage. The painting portrays the imperial audience of Gar Tongtsen Yülsung with Emperor Taizong of Tang, representing a historical testimony to the friendly relations between the Han and Tibetan peoples.

Artistically, it combines scattered perspective and centralised composition, highlighting the emperor’s supreme status through figure scale and positioning while achieving visual balance. Its elegant and rich colour scheme uses yellow to emphasise imperial dignity and red for an auspicious atmosphere. The brush lines are fluent, smooth and vigorous, with fine, rhythmic contours that endow the figures with vivid expressiveness.

This painting carries exceptional historical and artistic significance. It authentically documents the key diplomatic event of the Tang‑Tibetan marriage alliance, reflecting the costumes, rituals, customs and social life of the High Tang period, and stands as an important record of friendly exchanges between the Tang Dynasty and the Tibetan Empire.
