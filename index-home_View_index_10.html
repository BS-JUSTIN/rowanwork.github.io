Product Detail

Product Tags

The exhibits are mainly treasures of the Qing Dynasty court, and most of them are made by the construction office of the Internal Affairs Office and tributes from all over the world. The materials cover gold, silver, jade, emerald, pearl, red coral and various gems. The craft is a great achievement of silk, inlay, silk, enamel and jade carving, representing the highest level of handicrafts in the Qing Dynasty.

1.Ruby 

   Craft: natural ruby raw stone, rich in colour and full, transparent texture, uncarved, retaining natural texture.

   Culture: The natural gemstones collected by the court in the early Qing Dynasty are a symbol of imperial power and wealth, reflecting the royal family's cherishment of rare materials.

2.Green squis

   Process: It is carved with high-quality jade, using round carving and transparent carving techniques. The shape of the dragon is agile, the jade texture is shiny, and the colour is emerald green.

   Culture: Dragon is a traditional auspicious beast, implying auspiciousness. This piece is a court jade belt hook in the Qing Dynasty, which has both practical and display functions, representing the peak level of jade carving technology in the Qianlong period.

3.Copper gold-plated inlaid with gemstones

   Craft: The copper tire is gold-plated, and the engraving and inlay technology are used to inlay three large rubies and various gemstones, with complex and gorgeous patterns.

   Culture: For the leader of the belt of officials in the Qing Dynasty, the gem inlay and gold plating technology highlight the identity level of the wearer, which is the materialised embodiment of the official uniform system in the Qing Dynasty.

4.Silver gold-plated dot emerald inlaid pearl double dragon pattern long hairpin

   Process: Silver tires are gold-plated as the bottom, and the dot emerald process is adopted (pasted with kingfisher feathers), inlaid with pearls and gemstones. The shape of the double dragon is symmetrical and exquisite, and the colour of the dot emerald is bright.

   Culture: For the headdress of concubines in the Qing Dynasty, the double dragon pattern symbolises imperial power and auspiciousness. The green craft is the iconic technique of court jewellery in the Qing Dynasty, which has now been lost due to the protection of birds.

5.Silver gold-plated dot emerald inlaid gemstone double happy bat bell pattern hairpin

   Craft: silver tire gold-plated, green craft paving the background colour, inlaid with pearls, rubies, "double happiness", "bat" and "gong" pattern combination, implying "blessing and happiness".

   Culture: The headdress worn for the wedding or festival of the concubine in the Qing Dynasty. The pattern and colour follow the etiquette, reflecting the exquisite and auspicious style of the court aesthetics of the Daoguang period.

6.Malachite inlaid jewellery Penglai Wonderland

  Craft: With malachite as the base, inlaid with coral, jade, pearls, gemstones and other materials, and using stacking and inlay techniques to create a three-dimensional scene of Penglai Wonderland.

  Culture: Penglai Wonderland is the image of the traditional Taoist fairy mountain. This piece is the court display bonsai, symbolising longevity and auspiciousness, and representing the culmination of the court craft in the Qing Dynasty.

7.Carved wooden handle inlaid bead stone gold bat pattern three inlaid Ruyi

   Craft: The wooden handle adopts transparent carving and relief techniques, inlaid with jade, gemstones and gold ornaments. The "bat" pattern implies "blessing", and the three-inlaid Ruyi is the classic form of Ruyi in the Qing Dynasty.

   Culture: Ruyi is an important object of court etiquette and display in the Qing Dynasty, symbolising "all the best". This piece has both practical and aesthetic value, reflecting the delicate style of the Daoguang period.

8.Gold-inlaid jewellery long square box

   Craft: Gold tire as the bottom, using engraving and inlay technology, inlaid with pearls and gemstones, and the lid of the box is painted with Buddhist themes. The craft is complicated and the colours are gorgeous.

   Culture: The utensils for the Qing Dynasty court to display sutra scrolls or treasures, gold jewellery and gemstone inlays highlight the royal luxury, and Buddhist painting reflects the Qing Dynasty court's respect for Tibetan Buddhism.
