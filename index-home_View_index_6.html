Gu Kaizhi (active in the 4th century CE), courtesy name Changkang, and childhood name Hutou, was a native of Wuxi in Lingling (present-day Wuxi, Jiangsu Province). At the beginning of the Yixi reign period, he held the post of Cavalier Attendant-in-ordinary. A man of profound learning and diverse talents, he excelled in poetry, fu-style prose, and calligraphy, and was especially renowned for painting. He mastered various genres, including figure painting, Buddhist iconography, birds and beasts, and landscapes. In his time, he was celebrated for the "Three Uniquenesses": uniqueness of talent, uniqueness of painting, and uniqueness of eccentricity.


As a painter, he studied under Wei Xie. His brushwork was characterized by fine, vigorous, and continuous lines, likened to "a silkworm spinning its thread" or "flowing clouds and running water," emerging with natural grace. He was particularly famous for the art of dotting the iris when painting figures, stating: "The beauty or plainness of the four limbs has little to do with the subtlety of the portrayal; the capture of spirit and likeness lies precisely in the pupil of the eye (a colloquial term in the Six Dynasties meaning 'this,' referring to the eyeball)."


Tang Zhang Yanyuan commented on his paintings: "The conception precedes the brush; when the brush is laid down, the conception lingers on." A master of painting theory, he authored several influential works, including On Painting, In Praise of the Famous Painters of the Wei and Jin Dynasties, and Notes on Painting the Yuntai Mountain. His famous theoretical propositions, such as "transcending the mundane to attain the marvelous" and "using form to depict spirit," exerted a profound and lasting influence on the development of Chinese painting.


The Luoshen Fu Scroll

Basic information: It is said that it is the work of Gu Kai of the Eastern Jin Dynasty, and it is actually copied from the Song Dynasty; the silk book is colour, 27.1cm in length and 572.8cm in width.

Creative blueprint: According to Cao Zhi's "Luo Shen Fu" of the Three Kingdoms, it depicts the love between Cao Zhi and Luo Shen who meet, fall in love and part.

Picture content: segmented narrative, Cao Zhiluo meets Luo Shen, Luo Shen hides and appears from time to time; later Luo Shen drove away the six dragon cloud car, and looks back reluctantly.

Artistic characteristics: the composition is coherent, and the characters appear repeatedly according to the plot; the colour is rich, the painting method is ancient and clumsy, and the mountains, rocks and trees are filled without squidless, with the early landscape characteristics of "people are greater than mountains, and water cannot spread".

 Inscription imprint: At the beginning of the introduction, there is the inscription of Qianlong in the Qing Dynasty, and the inscriptions of all the Yuan and Ming Dynasties are all fake.


Biographies of Exemplary Women: Wise and Virtuous Ladies

It is a light-colored painting on silk, 25.8 cm high and 417.8 cm long.

Based on Biographies of Exemplary Women by Liu Xiang (Western Han), the scroll depicts stories of wise and virtuous women for moral education. The painting features vigorous lines and light ink washes to model forms, with figures and costumes reflecting Han dynasty styles. It embodies Gu Kaizhi’s theory of “capturing the spirit” in portraiture.
